A Users' Guide to Uncovering Worker and Firm Effects: The ABC of AKM

A practical introduction to the AKM methodology for decomposing wage dispersion into worker and firm components using linked employer-employee data. This guide covers the model, estimator, empirical findings, bias issues, and frontier extensions.

1

Linked Employer-Employee Data

  • Track the identity of workers and firms as workers move across employers
  • Increasingly widely available despite initial confidentiality barriers
  • Enable study of worker heterogeneity, firm heterogeneity, sorting, and wage dispersion
  • Share a common structure that makes the AKM approach applicable across settings

2

The Core Insight of AKM

  • Firm wage differences may reflect firm pay policies or worker composition
  • A high mean wage could mean the firm pays well (high \(\psi^j\)) or employs high-ability workers (high \(\alpha^i\))
  • AKM uses job mobility to separately identify worker and firm components
  • Once identified, these components quantify the roles of heterogeneity and sorting in wage dispersion

3

The AKM Model: Key Components

  • Worker effect \(\alpha^i\): persistent, worker-specific determinant of earnings (e.g., ability, task speed)
  • Firm effect \(\psi^j\): firm-specific wage premium applying to any worker at that firm
  • In logs, wages are additively separable: \(\alpha^i + \psi^j\)
  • Holding the firm fixed, higher-\(\alpha^i\) workers earn proportionally more
  • Holding the worker fixed, pay differences across firms equal \(\psi^1 - \psi^2\)

4

How Job Mobility Identifies the Components

  • When a worker moves from firm 1 to firm 2, the wage change equals \(\psi^2 - \psi^1\)
  • This differential is the same for all workers and constant over time
  • A worker moving to a lower-\(\psi^j\) firm experiences the same percentage drop as any other mover
  • The wage gap between two workers is preserved across firms
  • AKM leverages these patterns, extracted from linked data, to separately recover \(\alpha^i\) and \(\psi^j\)

5

The Econometric Model

$$Y_{it}(j) = X_{it}\beta + \alpha_i + \psi_j + U_{it}$$

  • \(Y_{it}(j)\): potential wage of worker \(i\) at firm \(j\) in period \(t\)
  • \(X_{it}\beta\): observed covariates (e.g., experience)
  • \(U_{it}\): idiosyncratic shock
  • Observed wage: \(Y_{it} = Y_{it}(j_{it})\) — only one firm observed per period
  • Recovering potential wages in all firms is a formidable extrapolation problem, solved by AKM's structure

6

The Exogenous Mobility Assumption

  • The model does not restrict how matches \((j_{it})\) relate to \(\alpha^i\) and \(\psi^j\) — sorting is allowed
  • However, \(U_{it}\) must be uncorrelated with all worker–firm matches in the data
  • Under this assumption, average wage differences of movers directly identify firm components
  • The assumption has been debated and remains controversial
  • Diagnostics and extensions will be discussed later

7

The AKM Estimator: Scale of the Problem

  • Typical datasets: hundreds of thousands or millions of workers and firms
  • Same number of worker- and firm-specific parameters to estimate
  • Initially challenging, but efficient algorithms are now well understood
  • State-of-the-art package: pytwoway

8

Connectivity and Identification

  • Right-hand-side variables are linearly dependent — naive regression fails
  • Level normalization: set average \(\psi^j\) to zero (only \(\alpha^i + \psi^j\) is identified)
  • Connectivity: firm \(\psi^j\) is identified only within connected sets of the worker–firm graph
  • A firm hiring/firing no one cannot be separated from its workers' \(\alpha^i\)
  • Standard practice: focus on the largest connected set (most workers and moderate/large firms)

9

The Connected Set Algorithm

  1. Start by including one worker in the set
  2. Include all firms where that worker has been employed
  3. Include all workers employed at those firms
  4. Repeat until the set stops growing
  5. In many applications, the largest connected set contains most workers and firms
  6. Typically leaves out some small firms and their workers

10

The Zigzag Algorithm

  • Instead of joint estimation, recover parameters one at a time iteratively:
  • Given \(\psi^j\) and \(\beta\): estimate \(\alpha^i\) as worker-specific averages
  • Given \(\alpha^i\) and \(\beta\): estimate \(\psi^j\) as firm-specific averages
  • Given \(\alpha^i\) and \(\psi^j\): estimate \(\beta\) by standard OLS
  • Iterate until convergence — fast and memory-efficient (Guimaraes and Portugal, 2010)
  • Exploits sparsity: workers visit only a handful of firms

11

Variance Decomposition

$$\operatorname{Var}(Y_{it} - X_{it}\beta) = \operatorname{Var}(\alpha_i) + \operatorname{Var}(\psi_{j_{it}}) + 2\operatorname{Cov}(\alpha_i, \psi_{j_{it}}) + \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_{it})$$

  • Worker variance: dispersion in permanent worker ability
  • Firm variance: dispersion in firm pay policies (zero if all firms pay identically)
  • Covariance: measures sorting — positive if high-\(\alpha^i\) workers match with high-\(\psi^j\) firms
  • Idiosyncratic variance: transitory shocks
  • If workers moved randomly, covariance would be zero

12

Cross-Sectional Findings: The US

Song et al. (2019), US 2007–2013, log annual earnings:

  • Between-firm variance: 39% of total variance
  • AKM decomposition sharpens the picture:
  • Worker variance: 59%
  • Firm variance: 10%
  • Covariance: 13%
  • Residual: 18%
  • Firm variance and covariance arise solely from between-firm dispersion
  • Worker variance reflects both within- and between-firm dispersion

13

Changes Over Time: Germany

Card et al. (2013), German daily wages, 1985–1991 vs. 2002–2009:

  • Total variance increased by 11.1 percentage points
  • Decomposition of the increase:
  • Worker variance: 39%
  • Firm variance: 24%
  • Covariance: 34%
  • Residual: small
  • Song et al. (2019) find a similar pattern for the US (1978–2013), with a substantial covariance increase

14

Applications Beyond Labor Economics

  • Health economics: Finkelstein, Gentzkow, and Williams (2016) — patient migration across regions separates patient-specific from place-specific drivers of health care utilization; both matter, covariance is positive
  • Corporate finance: Amiti and Weinstein (2018) — separate bank-specific supply shocks from firm-specific demand shocks using matched lending data; bank shocks significantly affect aggregate investment
  • Other domains: education, international trade, and more

15

The Bias Problem: Sources of Noise

  • Estimates of \(\alpha^i\) and \(\psi^j\) are contaminated by substantial noise
  • Firm differences are identified by wages of movers — the number of movers is key
  • Weakly connected firms (few movers) yield especially noisy estimates
  • Noise does not average out for variances and covariances → bias
  • Bias can be substantial even with millions of workers and firms
  • Direction: firm variance overstated, covariance understated

16

Diagnosing Bias: Sub-Sampling Plots

Andrews et al. (2012) sub-sampling experiment:

  1. Start with large, well-connected firms
  2. Randomly remove job movers within each firm
  3. Plot the quantity of interest vs. average movers per firm

Findings (German data): - In smaller, less connected samples: correlation is low or negative - In larger, more connected samples: correlation is positive and large - Clear evidence that AKM-based covariance/correlation estimates are biased downward

17

Bias Correction Methods

Fixed-effects methods: - Exploit linear regression algebra to construct bias estimates - Andrews et al. (2008): trace-based correction assuming common shock variance - Kline, Saggio, and Sølvsten (2020): extension allowing unrestricted variance heterogeneity

Correlated random-effects methods: - Model the distribution of \(\alpha^i\) and \(\psi^j\); estimate distribution parameters - Bayesian interpretation: prior → posterior (Empirical Bayes) - Woodcock (2015); Bonhomme et al. (2023); Cheng, Ho, and Schorfheide (2025) - Both available in pytwoway

18

Impact of Bias Correction: Cross-Country Evidence

Bonhomme et al. (2023), five countries, 6-year panels:

Quantity Uncorrected AKM Bias-Corrected
Firm variance share 12%–24% 5%–17%
Covariance share negative in 3/5 countries 4%–13% (all positive)
  • Confirms: without correction, firm variance is overstated, covariance is understated
  • Correction can materially change conclusions about sorting and firm pay policies

19

Limitations of AKM Assumptions

AKM relies on two key assumptions:

  • Additivity: \(\alpha^i + \psi^j\) — rules out interactions \(\alpha^i \times \psi^j\)
  • At odds with Becker (1973) sorting theory where complementarities drive matching
  • Exogenous mobility: \(U_{it}\) unrelated to matching process
  • Cannot account for leaving due to negative wage shocks
  • No effect of past firms on current wage (rules out firm-specific human capital)
  • Inconsistent with search models with wage posting (Postel-Vinay and Robin, 2002)

20

Empirical Violations of AKM Assumptions

  • Card et al. (2013) event study: additivity may be a reasonable approximation; no pre-mobility "dip" alleviates exogenous mobility concerns
  • But: different groups (men/women, racial groups) are affected by different firm effects (Card, Cardoso, and Kline 2016; Gerard et al. 2021)
  • \(\psi^j\) is not the same for all workers — violates a core AKM assumption
  • The static nature of AKM is difficult to reconcile with dynamic models of job mobility

21

Extensions and Structural Models

  • Bonhomme, Lamadon, and Manresa (2019): interaction effects, dynamic past-firm effects, endogenous mobility — log-additive approximates variance well, but interactions and dynamics are present
  • Abowd, McKinney, and Schmutte (2019): endogenous mobility via past shocks influencing leave decisions
  • Structural models with two-sided heterogeneity: Hagedorn et al. (2017), Sorkin (2018), Lamadon et al. (2022), Lentz et al. (2023), Lamadon et al. (2024), Borovičková and Shimer (2024)
  • Beyond labor: spillovers/team production, manager-task complementarities (Crippa 2025)

22

Key Takeaways

  • AKM is a powerful, widely used tool for decomposing wage dispersion using linked employer-employee data
  • The additive model \(\alpha^i + \psi^j\) with exogenous mobility is simple in appearance but sophisticated in implications
  • Best practices: focus on the connected set, use the zigzag algorithm, always check for bias via sub-sampling plots
  • Bias correction is essential for variance decompositions — uncorrected estimates overstate firm effects and understate sorting
  • Active frontier: relaxing additivity, allowing dynamics, incorporating endogenous mobility, and extending to non-labor settings
