Steam, Steel, and Infinite Minds

Every era is shaped by its miracle material. Steel forged the Gilded Age. Semiconductors switched on the Digital Age. Now AI has arrived as infinite minds. Those who master the material define the era.

1

The Pattern of Miracle Materials

  • In the 1850s, Andrew Carnegie ran through muddy Pittsburgh as a telegraph boy
  • Six in ten Americans were farmers
  • Within two generations, Carnegie and his peers forged the modern world
  • Horses gave way to railroads, candlelight to electricity, iron to steel
  • Since then, work shifted from factories to offices

2

From Telegraph Boy to Software CEO

  • Today's industry town is San Francisco, building tools for millions of knowledge workers
  • Everyone is talking about AGI
  • But most of the two billion desk workers have yet to feel it
  • Key questions: What will knowledge work look like soon? What happens when the org chart absorbs minds that never sleep?

3

Driving to the Future via the Rearview Window

  • Early phone calls were concise like telegrams
  • Early movies looked like filmed stage plays
  • This is what Marshall McLuhan called "driving to the future via the rearview window."
  • Today's most popular AI mimics Google search boxes
  • We're deep in the uncomfortable transition phase that happens with every new technology shift

4

Individuals: From Bicycles to Cars

  • The first glimpses appear with programmers — the high priests of knowledge work
  • Steve Jobs called personal computers "bicycles for the mind" in the 1980s
  • A decade later came the "information superhighway" — the internet
  • But most knowledge work is still human-powered
  • It's like pedaling bicycles on the autobahn
  • With AI agents, top engineers have graduated from riding a bicycle to driving a car

5

The 10× Programmer Becomes 30-40×

  • Co-founder Simon was a 10× programmer, but rarely writes code now
  • He orchestrates three or four AI coding agents at once
  • They don't just type faster — they think
  • He queues tasks before lunch or bed, letting agents work while he's away
  • He's become a manager of infinite minds

6

Two Barriers for Knowledge Workers

  • Coding agents work well — why is general knowledge work harder?
  • First: context fragmentation
  • Coding context lives in one place: the IDE, the repo, the terminal
  • Knowledge work is scattered across dozens of tools
  • Humans are the glue, stitching with copy-paste and tab-switching
  • Second: verifiability
  • Code can be verified with tests and errors
  • How do you verify a well-managed project or a good strategy memo?
  • Humans still need to supervise and show what "good" looks like

7

The Red Flag Act Problem

  • The Red Flag Act of 1865 required a person to walk ahead of every vehicle
  • Programming agents taught us that "human-in-the-loop" isn't always desirable
  • It's like inspecting every bolt on a factory line, or walking in front of a car
  • We want humans to supervise from a leveraged point, not be in the loop
  • Once context is consolidated and work is verifiable, billions will go from pedaling → driving → self-driving

8

Organizations: Steel and Steam

  • Companies are a recent invention — most were workshops of a dozen people
  • Now multinationals have hundreds of thousands of employees
  • Communication infrastructure (brains + meetings + messages) buckles under exponential load
  • We solve this with hierarchy, process, and documentation
  • We've been solving an industrial-scale problem with human-scale tools — like building a skyscraper with wood

9

AI Is Steel for Organizations

  • Before steel, buildings were limited to six or seven floors — iron was brittle and heavy
  • Steel is strong yet malleable: lighter frames, thinner walls, dozens of stories
  • AI is steel for organizations:
  • Maintains context across workflows without the noise
  • Human communication no longer has to be the load-bearing wall
  • Weekly two-hour alignment → five-minute async review
  • Three-level approval → decisions in minutes
  • Companies can truly scale without the degradation we've accepted as inevitable

10

The Steam Engine Lesson

  • Early textile factories sat next to rivers, powered by waterwheels
  • When steam engines arrived, owners swapped waterwheels for steam — kept everything else the same
  • Productivity gains were modest
  • The breakthrough: decouple from water entirely, build near workers and ports, redesign factories around steam
  • Later, electricity further decentralized power to individual machines
  • Productivity exploded — the Second Industrial Revolution took off

11

Still Swapping Waterwheels

  • We're still in the "swap out the waterwheel" phase
  • AI chatbots bolted onto existing tools
  • We haven't reimagined what organizations look like when old constraints dissolve
  • At Notion: alongside 1,000 employees, 700+ agents handle repetitive work
  • Meeting notes, tribal knowledge synthesis, IT requests
  • Customer feedback logging, new hire onboarding, weekly status reports
  • This is just baby steps — real gains are limited only by imagination and inertia

12

Economies: From Florence to Megacities

  • Until recently, cities were human-scaled — you could walk across Florence in forty minutes
  • Steel frames made skyscrapers possible; steam powered railways
  • Elevators, subways, highways followed
  • Cities exploded: Tokyo, Chongqing, Dallas
  • These aren't just bigger Florences — they're different ways of living
  • More disorienting and anonymous, but offering more opportunity and freedom

13

The Knowledge Economy's Transformation

  • Knowledge work represents nearly half of America's GDP
  • Most still operates at human scale: teams of dozens, meetings and email, buckling past a few hundred people
  • We've built Florences with stone and wood
  • When AI agents come online at scale, we'll build Tokyos:
  • Organizations spanning thousands of agents and humans
  • Workflows running continuously across time zones
  • Decisions synthesized with the right amount of human oversight
  • Faster, more leveraged — but more disorienting at first
  • Old rhythms (weekly meeting, quarterly planning, annual review) may stop making sense

14

Beyond the Waterwheels

  • Every miracle material required people to stop seeing the world via the rearview window
  • Carnegie looked at steel and saw city skylines
  • Lancashire mill owners looked at steam and saw factories free from rivers
  • We're still bolting chatbots onto workflows designed for humans
  • We need to stop asking AI to be merely our copilots
  • We need to imagine knowledge work reinforced with steel, busywork delegated to minds that never sleep

Steel. Steam. Infinite minds. The next skyline is there, waiting for us to build it.
